# Week 2: Why "learn to code" was always the wrong advice

**Subject:** Why "learn to code" was always the wrong advice

---

For the last decade, the default career advice for ambitious professionals was "learn to code." Build something. Ship it.

The advice wasn't wrong about building. It was wrong about what to build, and who should build it.

**The uncomfortable truth:** Most software built by career-switchers solves problems that don't exist or solves real problems for buyers who can't be reached. The code works. The business doesn't.

The missing piece was never technical skill. It was market context.

A 15-year operations manager knows more about where workflows break than any developer who's never worked in ops. A finance director understands reporting bottlenecks in ways that no outside founder could replicate. That context, earned through years of actual work, is the most undervalued asset in the market right now.

**What's changed:** Modern tools make the building part accessible without writing code. The real barrier was always knowing what to build, who to build it for, and how to reach those buyers. That's where domain expertise becomes an unfair advantage.

**The distribution insight:** The strongest distribution channels for expertise-based solutions are the professional communities you're already part of. The Slack groups, the LinkedIn connections, the industry events. You don't need to build an audience. You need to activate the network you already have with a clear, specific offer.

### 3 moves you can make this week:

1. **List the 5 professional communities you're most active in.** Slack groups, LinkedIn groups, industry forums, local meetups. These are your first distribution channels.

2. **For the problem you identified last week, write a one-paragraph description of the solution.** Not the features. The outcome. "Cuts new hire onboarding from 90 days to 30 days" is better than "automated onboarding platform."

3. **Share that one paragraph with 3 trusted peers and ask one question:** "Would you pay for this?" Not "is this a good idea?" but "would you pay?" The answers are very different.
